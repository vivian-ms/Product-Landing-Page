White Tea

  • Least processed of the true teas and undergoes only one simple manufacturing process
  • Light yellow in color but some varieties can have hints of green
  • One of the more mild-flavored true teas
  • Has floral and fruity undertones that add a natural sweetness
  • The two popular varieties are Silver Needle and White Peony

Green Tea

  • Green tea leaves undergo a multi-step production procsss but like white tea leaves, don't undergo an oxidation process
  • Most are light green or slightly yellow in color
  • Chinese green teas are pan fried to produce roasted nutty flavors
  • Japanese green teas are steamed to preserve vegetal and herbaceous flavors
  • Popular varieties include gunpowder, matcha, genmaicha, and sencha green teas

Oolong Tea

  • Known as 'wulong tea' in China
  • Semi-oxidized tea that's deep amber or light green in color and offers a floral flavor with a smooth finish that results in medium-bodied taste
  • Oolong teas are produced solely in China and Taiwan and are often named based on the region they're cultivated
  • The least oxidized oolong teas are known as pouchongs and one of the most heavily oxidized oolong tea is Da Hong Pao

Black Tea

  • The most popular type of tea in the West
  • Black teas are most commonly produced in China and on the Indian continent
  • The taste can be vastly different depending on the specific tea leaves and blend
  • The growing regions in India that produce the largest amounts of black tea include Assam and Darjeeling
  • The third largest growing region is Nilgiri in Sri Lanki, formerly known as Ceylon

About Us

Drinking a cup of tea everyday can help promote good health. There are thousands of different types of teas ranging from the classic true tea varieties to endless herbal and spiced teas.

Here at Real Good Tea, we sell true teas, which are made using leaves from the tea plant known as Camellia sinensis. Teas made from any other plants are not technically true teas. True teas can be divided into 4 major categories: white tea, green tea, oolong tea, and black tea. Generally, these categories refer to how much the tea is oxidized (or fermented).

The leaves of Camellia sinensis contain naturally occurring caffeine that's actually higher in concentration than in coffee beans. Coffee is only stronger than tea because it's more concentrated when brewed. The actual caffeine content in a cup of true tea varies largely depending on the production method.
